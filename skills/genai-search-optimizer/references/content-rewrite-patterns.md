# Content Rewrite Patterns

Use these patterns when content scores as commodity, AI-first, or generic. Each pattern moves the page toward non-commodity, people-first content. Apply only the patterns that fit the page; do not force all of them.

## Pattern 1: Replace the listicle preamble with a personal stake

**Before:**

> "Buying your first home is one of the biggest decisions of your life. Here are 7 tips to help you navigate the process."

**After:**

> "We waived the inspection on our first home and ended up replacing the sewer line three weeks later. Here is what I would tell anyone deciding whether to skip an inspection on a hot market offer."

Why it works: the rewrite signals first-hand experience and immediate stakes. The original is commodity preamble that any blog could produce.

## Pattern 2: Lead with the surprising finding, not the definition

**Before:**

> "Core Web Vitals are a set of metrics that measure user experience on a webpage..."

**After:**

> "After we cut INP by 180 ms on the checkout page, conversion only moved 0.4 percent. Here is what actually mattered for our funnel, and why CWV scores can mislead."

Why it works: a real number from a real project signals experience. The textbook definition can be linked, not retold.

## Pattern 3: Convert pros and cons fluff into a decision matrix from your data

**Before:**

> "PostgreSQL has many advantages: it's open source, it has strong community support, it scales well... However, it also has some disadvantages..."

**After:**

> A two-column matrix listing the actual decisions you made on a recent project: workload type, dataset size, replication needs, ops budget, and which database won and why.

Why it works: a matrix grounded in named decisions is non-commodity. Generic pros and cons lists are exactly what AI summarization devalues.

## Pattern 4: Replace generic case study with a named, dated, first-hand account

**Before:**

> "A leading e-commerce company saw a 30% increase in conversions after implementing personalization."

**After:**

> "When we shipped product recommendations on the cart page at \[company name\] in March 2026, average order value moved from \$48 to \$53 over six weeks. Here is the exact A/B setup and what the variant looked like."

Why it works: named, dated, first-hand evidence is what Google's helpful content guidance calls out as a signal of expertise and experience.

## Pattern 5: Pull the expert quote forward as the opening claim

**Before:**

> "Many experts believe that AI will transform SEO. According to a recent survey..."

**After:**

> "Google's own guidance says that AEO and GEO are still SEO, and that llms.txt files and content chunking do not help with AI Overviews. Here is what that means for the SEO budget you are planning for Q3."

Why it works: cite the authoritative source up front and apply it to a concrete decision the reader has to make.

## Pattern 6: Replace the keyword-stuffed H2 with a question the reader actually asks

**Before:**

> "## Best AI SEO Tools for AI Overviews and Generative Engine Optimization in 2026"

**After:**

> "## Do you actually need a tool for this, or can your existing SEO stack handle it?"

Why it works: the question-shaped heading matches how readers think and how AI search summarizes intent. The keyword-stuffed heading reads like it was written for a 2014 ranking algorithm.

## Pattern 7: Replace the "ultimate guide" with a focused decision aid

**Before:**

> "The Ultimate Guide to Migrating from Universal Analytics to GA4"

**After:**

> "Three GA4 migration decisions that cost us hours in week one. Make them before you start the migration."

Why it works: a focused page that delivers a small number of decisions completely is more useful than a broad page that covers many topics shallowly. Google has stated that page length should match audience and subject; "ultimate guide" framing usually inflates length without depth.

## Pattern 8: Replace synthesized "best practices" with what your team actually did

**Before:**

> "Best practices for API documentation include providing clear examples, using consistent terminology..."

**After:**

> "We rewrote our API docs last quarter. Here are the three changes that cut support tickets the most, and the two that looked good in design review but had no measurable effect."

Why it works: "what we tried and what actually moved the needle" is exactly the experience signal Google's helpful content guidance asks for.

## Pattern 9: Replace the AI-generated outline with the outline that fits the original argument

**Before:** the symmetric H2 structure that LLM drafts produce by default (Introduction, What Is X, Why X Matters, Benefits of X, How to Implement X, Conclusion).

**After:** the outline that fits the argument, even if it is asymmetric. Some sections are long because they carry the unique insight. Some sections do not exist because they would be filler.

Why it works: AI-generated outlines are recognizable and read as commodity. Rewriting to the actual argument signals human authorship and editorial judgment.

## Pattern 10: Replace the closing summary with a next-step the reader can actually take

**Before:**

> "In conclusion, optimizing for AI search requires a multi-faceted approach..."

**After:**

> "Audit one page against the criteria in this post this week. Compare what you find to your last three months of Search Console data and decide which fix to ship first."

Why it works: a concrete next step is more useful and more memorable than a restatement of the introduction. AI summaries do the restatement job for free; your closing should add something that summary cannot.

## Application notes

- Do not apply every pattern to every page. Pick the two or three that fit.
- For AI-assisted content, run the rewrite, then run the helpful content self-check (see Search Central guidance) before publishing.
- Preserve the reader as the audience. Patterns that read better to a human almost always read better to AI summarization too. The reverse is not true.
